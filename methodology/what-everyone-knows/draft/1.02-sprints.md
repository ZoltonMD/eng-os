## II. Sprints

Have you run a marathon? I think it's one of those things which is not necessary to try yourself to understand that it's 
very difficult and very demanding. It's **What Everyone Knows**. Most people can't do it physically (me, for example). 
To finish a marathon you must be in good shape, have good health, and be properly trained. But if we take a look at your 
entire running history, and sum all segments you ever ran, what will we get? How many marathons have we run in our lives? 
Even without special preparations and training, every month we cover huge distances moving with short segments every day. 
It's a very simple logic and it works in many areas of your life. 

Of course it's not the same as a real marathon. But from the point of view of the work done it's the same work that is 
done for the same time. And sometimes even more, because you start every new chunk full of energy and motivation.

---

### Example 1
Imagine a common task: you need to upgrade the “Service A” from version `2.x` to `3.x`. On the surface, it’s standard 
ticket that might simply read:  

` • Upgrade Service A to 3.x across all environments.`  

But let's look closer. This is a major update. In our world, that likely means these versions are incompatible and 
require data or metadata migration. Once that migration is finished, there may be no way to roll back. What does this 
imply? It means that we should be well-prepared; blindly updating production is a recipe for disaster. Suddenly, a 
“trivial” task becomes complex and potentially dangerous. Now, we need to design rollout and rollback plans, test the 
upgrade path, update staging, hit production, and then review all related documentation - the list goes on.  

This is exactly how I approached things in the beginning. I perceived such tasks as monoliths. While working on one part, 
I would stumble upon new questions that needed solving. I’d switch to those new questions, falling into a chaotic, 
uncontrollable whirlwind. Of course, I produced some results every day, but by the evening, I felt a deep sense of 
frustration. I couldn’t measure my progress. I had a persistent feeling that the task was growing faster than I could 
resolve it, because I was uncovering more questions that I was providing answers to.  
So, let’s perform a post-mortem and try to figure out exactly what went wrong.  

---

### What is wrong with the "Example 1"
First things first. To fix a problem we must know what is actually wrong with the system.    

**Context switching.** First, monoliths, or elephants as it is called in time-management, cause more context switches. 
We already spoke about the harmful influence of context switches on our productivity in the previous chapter (and will 
more in the future), and I will not dwell on this for long.
Big tasks have large and complicated context models. Which demands a longer time to load and unload them from a working 
memory. Also, if you are working on a big task the whole day, then any interruption, any incoming message is a 
distraction for you which causes context switching. Every time your brain must remember when it had stopped, reload 
the context and so on. It takes a lot of time.

**The Result Measurement.** When you work on a monolith task, it’s hard to understand how far you have come during 
the day, because you are permanently switching from one thought to another. Working on configuration you see one of 
the properties and remember that you didn’t describe it in documentation. Then, you switch to documentation, write down 
the part you just remembered, and return back to the configuration. As a result, at the very end of the day you have 
many small outcomes from different parts of the task. This result is very difficult to measure. 
If you can’t see a clear result for some time it can become a demotivating factor. This leads to a chronic 
dissatisfaction with your work. This dissatisfaction lowers motivation, reduces productivity, and eventually leads to 
burnout. I presume many of us are familiar with this feeling: you’ve worked hard the whole day, you are exhausted, yet 
at the very end of the day, you don’t see a significant outcome. You feel like nothing was actually done.  
It’s a horrible feeling. I know it well.

**Monolith Paralysis.**
