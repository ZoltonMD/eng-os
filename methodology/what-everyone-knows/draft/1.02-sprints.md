## II. Sprints

Have you run a marathon? I think it's one of those things which is not necessary to try yourself to understand that it's very difficult and very demanding. It's **What Everyone Knows**. Most people can't do it physically (me, for example). To finish a marathon you must be in good shape, have good health, and be properly trained. But if we take a look at your entire running history, and sum all segments you ever ran, what will we get? How many marathons have we run in our lives? Even without special preparations and training, every month we cover huge distances moving with short segments every day. It's a very simple logic and it works in many areas of your life. 

Of course it's not the same as a real marathon. But from the point of view of the work done it's the same work that is done for the same time. And sometimes even more, because you start every new chunk full of energy and motivation.

---

### Example 1
Imagine a common task: you need to upgrade the “Service A” from version `2.x` to `3.x`. On the surface, it’s standard ticket that might simply read:  

` • Upgrade Service A to 3.x across all environments.`  

But let's look closer. This is a major update. In our world, that likely means these versions are incompatible and require data or metadata migration. Once that migration is finished, there may be no way to roll back. What does this imply? It means that we should be well-prepared; blindly updating production is a recipe for disaster. Suddenly, a “trivial” task becomes complex and potentially dangerous. Now, we need to design rollout and rollback plans, test the upgrade path, update staging, hit production, and then review all related documentation - the list goes on.  

This is exactly how I approached things in the beginning. I perceived such tasks as monoliths. While working on one part, I would stumble upon new questions that needed solving. I’d twitch to those new questions, falling into a chaotic, uncontrollable whirlwind. Of course, I produced some results every day, but by the evening, I felt a deep sense of frustration. I couldn’t measure my progress. I had a persistent feeling that the task was growing faster than I could resolve it, because I was uncovering more questions that I was providing answers to.  

This leads to a chronic dissatisfaction with your work. It lowers motivation, reduces productivity, and eventually leads to burnout. I presume many of us are familiar with this feeling: you’ve worked hard the whole day, you are exhausted, yet at the very end of the day, you don’t see a significant outcome. You feel like nothing was actually done.  

It’s a horrible feeling. I know it well.  

So, let’s perform a post-mortem and try to figure out exactly what went wrong.
